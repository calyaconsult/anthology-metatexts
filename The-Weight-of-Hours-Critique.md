- **The Plotlessness That Isn’t Really Plotlessness**

Like a Godard or Resnais film, the narrative *pretends* to have no plot—just a woman walking, remembering, feeling. But underneath, there’s an almost mathematical pattern: returning to sites of memory, observing strangers, finding correspondences.
It’s not “plotless,” it’s **structured by recurrence**—the same way *L’Année dernière à Marienbad* loops gestures until emotion becomes geometry.
Rachel’s repetition of place and image (house → café → same table → same gestures) is textbook nouvelle vague: story replaced by rhythm.

---

- **The Philosophical Distance**

In classic nouvelle vague, emotion is never shown raw; it’s refracted through intellect. That’s exactly what’s happening here.
Rachel doesn’t *feel* her grief so much as **analyze** it—she treats it like a philosophical experiment in meaning, causality, and coincidence. Her observations about gentrification, ritual, repetition—all of that is Godardian detachment.
It’s *“mourning as discourse.”*

Even that last line—

> “You don’t bury the dead. You just learn to carry them in smaller boxes…”

- **The City as Psychological Mirror**

In nouvelle vague films, the city isn’t just setting—it’s the externalized mind of the protagonist. Paris in *Cléo*, Rome in *L’Eclisse*; here, Portland fills the same role.
Notice how the city “continues without her permission.” That’s the alienation of the modern individual—the realization that the world moves with or without your meaning attached to it.
It’s existential cinema on the page: the protagonist wandering through a world that’s moved on, watching her own irrelevance reflected in architecture.

---

- **The Fragmented Subject**

Rachel is not a traditional character with a psychological arc; she’s more like a collection of perspectives. Nouvelle vague protagonists often exist in pieces—observing themselves, commenting internally, drifting between identities.
Rachel’s constant self-surveillance (“She counted three minutes,” “She couldn’t remember what she ordered”) mimics the camera watching her *not act.*
This alienation—between self as actor and self as observer—is what gives the prose that cool, glassy unease.

- **The Anti-Catharsis**

In the nouvelle vague, catharsis is vulgar. The “moment of realization” is always withheld. The audience is asked to *think*, not to feel closure.
The excerpt ends in precisely that way: Rachel reaches a point of *non-resolution* that is presented as the only honest resolution. “It didn’t matter as much as she’d thought.” That line could have come straight from *Vivre sa vie* or *Le Mépris*.
The refusal of drama is itself the drama.
