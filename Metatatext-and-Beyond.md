### 1. Hypotext & Hypertext
If **Metatext** is commentary *about* a text, **Hypertext** is a new text that transforms or extends an earlier one.

*   **Hypotext**: The earlier, source text that serves as the foundation.
*   **Hypertext**: The later text that grafts itself onto the hypotext through imitation, parody, transformation, or extension.
*   **Relationship**: Transformation/Derivation.
*   **Example**:
    *   *Hypotext*: Homer's *The Odyssey*.
    *   *Hypertext*: James Joyce's *Ulysses* (a modern reimagining) or the Coen Brothers' film *O Brother, Where Art Thou?*
    *   *Note*: Unlike metatext (which talks *about* the Odyssey), hypertext *uses* the Odyssey to tell a new story.

### 2. Intertext
While paratext is on the threshold and metatext is commentary, **Intertext** is the actual presence of one text within another.

*   **Definition**: The concrete relationship where a text explicitly quotes, plagiarizes, alludes to, or echoes another text. It is the "co-presence" of two texts.
*   **Relationship**: Co-presence/Quotation.
*   **Example**:
    *   T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* is dense with **intertextuality**, containing direct quotes and fragments from Dante, Shakespeare, and the Upanishads woven directly into the poem's fabric.
    *   A character in a novel saying, "Frankly, my dear, I don't give a damn," creates an intertextual link to *Gone with the Wind*.

### 3. Architext
This is the most abstract level. If paratext tells you *how* to read a specific book, **Architext** tells you *what kind* of book it is before you even open it.

*   **Definition**: The set of general categories, genres, or types to which a text belongs. It is often signaled by the paratext (e.g., the label "A Novel" or "Sci-Fi" on the cover).
*   **Relationship**: Classification/Genre.
*   **Example**:
    *   When you pick up a book with a dark cover, a skull, and the word "Mystery" on the spine, the **architext** sets your expectation for a crime, a detective, and a resolution, even before you read a single word of the **text**.

### 4. Context
Moving slightly outside Genette's strict structuralism into broader cultural theory, **Context** is the environment in which the text exists.

*   **Definition**: The historical, social, political, and biographical circumstances surrounding the production and reception of the text.
*   **Relationship**: Environment/Situation.
*   **Example**:
    *   Reading George Orwell's *1984* without knowing the **context** of Stalinism and the rise of totalitarianism in the 20th century changes the meaning of the text significantly.

### 5. Subtext
Often confused with context, **Subtext** is the unspoken meaning *beneath* the literal words of the text.

*   **Definition**: The underlying themes, motives, or emotions that are implied but not explicitly stated. It is what the characters (or author) are thinking but not saying.
*   **Relationship**: Implication/Undertext.
*   **Example**:
    *   In a scene where two characters discuss the weather politely while actually negotiating a divorce, the dialogue is the **text**, but the tension and sadness are the **subtext**.

### 6. Posttext / Epitext (Genette's specific addition)
Genette actually split the concept of "outside the text" further. We covered **Paratext** (inside the volume), but he also defined:

*   **Epitext**: Any paratextual element located *outside* the physical volume of the book.
    *   **Examples**: Interviews with the author, private correspondence, diary entries, reviews in magazines, marketing campaigns, and authorial tweets.
    *   **Function**: Like paratext, it influences interpretation, but it exists in the public sphere rather than bound in the book.
